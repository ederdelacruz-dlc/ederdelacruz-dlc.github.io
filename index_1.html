III. The AI Thesis

Four videos.
No institutional backing. One argument.

Most AI-in-education content teaches tools. I spent my final year before retirement asking the harder question: what happens to the purpose of school when AI absorbs the work school was built around?

Conferences offer apps and prompts. Almost no one asks what happens to a student's sense of purpose when AI changes what school is for.
I propose structural changes: mentorship-centered teacher roles, project-based governance, schedules built around human needs rather than content delivery.
Inspired by the Alpha School model — AI handles core content in fewer hours a day, freeing the rest for what humans do best.
Published under my own name, with nothing to gain professionally because the gap in the conversation needed filling.

IV. Teaching Philosophy

Twenty-five years of thinking
about how people learn.

Three lenses on the same underlying conviction — click through.

The Actor-Director
Notice, Then Build
AI as the New Stage

My teaching philosophy centers on an actor-director frame: I set the stage, but the students are the ones who have to live inside it. A director who never lets the actors make a real choice produces a flat performance — and a teacher who scripts every moment produces students who can't think without a script.

My job has always been to design the conditions for real thinking to happen — then get out of the way. That cognitive engagement is what we will all need in an AI future.

Every program I've built started the same way: I noticed something was missing, sat with the discomfort of it for a while, and then built the thing that should have been there.

This isn't restlessness — it's a methodology.

The Writing Center, the horticulture course, the equity training series — none of them came with a mandate. All of them came from the same instinct: I see the gaps, and I become someone who can fill it.

The actor-director frame applies directly to AI. The question isn't whether students use AI — they will and they should. The harder question is what happens to a young person's sense of agency and purpose when the intellectual and creative work school has always promised them is increasingly something a model can do faster.

That's what the video series tries to answer. Not with a tools list but with a structural argument about what education is actually for.
